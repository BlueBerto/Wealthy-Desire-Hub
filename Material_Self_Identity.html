Material self-presentation and identity are deeply interconnected, with design and objects playing crucial roles in reflecting and shaping who we are.

According to John Heskett

A British writer and lecturer renowned for his insights on the economic, political, cultural, and human value of industrial design, design merges 'need' and 'desire' into practical objects that also embody the user's identity and aspirations through their form and decoration. Heskett, a professor at the Institute of Design at Illinois Institute of Technology, believed that everyday objects carry significant meanings and functions, often affecting us subconsciously. Whether at home, work, or leisure, the design of objects, from the smallest items like toothpicks to larger gadgets and cars, reflects personal identity.

According to Roland Barthes

The French critical theorist, was instrumental in exploring the relationships between people and objects, viewing objects as signs that convey messages beyond their practical value. In the 1950s, Barthes popularized semiology, the study of signs and symbols as elements of communicative behavior. He argued that everyday objects form part of a complex system of signs, allowing us to read meanings into people and places. In semiotic analysis, objects function as signifiers in the production of meaning. For instance, clothes serve the practical purpose of covering the body but also act as cultural signs conveying specific messages. Barthes posited that all things, whether verbal or visual, could be seen as a form of language, implying that even ordinary objects can be eloquent. Barthes introduced semiotics, examining how words, photographs, images, and objects act as a language to communicate ideas, associations, and feelings. He identified two elements of a sign: the signifier (its physical form, such as a diamond ring) and the signified (the mental concept it represents, such as engagement). Objects, therefore, reflect broader communicative practices and individual identities. This perspective suggests that objects function as complex systems of signs, enabling interpretations of people, places, and purposes. For example, advertising transforms products like perfumes into essences of allure, making users irresistible. People's possessions and surroundings often reflect their personalities, as the clothes they wear, the cars they drive, and their home furnishings express their identities. Thus, the objects surrounding individuals are inseparable from who they are, and signs differentiate one person or group from another.

The theory of the meaning of material possessions 4 types

  • Instrumental Functions:

    These relate to the functional properties of a product, essentially how useful or practical the item is in everyday life.

  • Social Symbolic Funtions:

    These signify personal qualities, social standing, group affiliation, and gender role. In other words, the items can communicate information about a person's identity and social position.

  • Categorical Functions:

    These refer to the extent to which material possessions may be used to communicate group membership and status, similar to social symbolic functions but more focused on broader social categories.

  • Self-Expressive Functions:

    These reflect a person's unique qualities, values, or attitudes, allowing individuals to express their individuality and personal identity through their possessions.

Economic Self Presentation and Identity

Econimic self-presentation and identity are intricate concepts shaped by individual experiences and societal influences. Economics, at its core, examines how people manage scarcity, utilize resources, and make decisions to enhance material well-being. Economic self-sufficiency denotes the capability of individuals and families to consistently fulfill their needs without external support.

Economic consciousness

The socialization and professional development individuals undergo, imbuing them with perceptions, attitudes, and opinions about economic objects and phenomena. This consciousness plays a pivotal role in human affairs, influencing how individuals interact with and perceive their economic environment. Central to economic identity is its foundation in social categorization. Individuals are often classified based on their economic status, whether categorized as rich, sustainable, or poor within society. This classification is influenced by factors such as professional growth, self-determination, and the motivational and value systems inherent to each person's personality.
